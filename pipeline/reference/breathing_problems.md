# Does Global Warming Cause Breathing Problems?

**Based on Science**

*In many regions, the warming climate is making air quality worse. Poorer air quality can cause or worsen breathing problems and respiratory illnesses.*

---

## The Short Answer

Yes. As the planet warms, the air we breathe is getting worse in many places. Hotter temperatures fuel the creation of smog, make wildfires more common, and extend allergy season — all of which can make it harder to breathe, especially for people who already have conditions like asthma or COPD (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease).

---

## How Heat Creates Harmful Smog

You've probably seen haze hanging over a city on a hot summer day. That haze often contains ground-level ozone — a harmful gas that forms when sunlight heats up pollution from car exhaust, wildfires, and industrial sources.

Here's the key: the hotter and sunnier it gets, the faster this pollution forms. And on hot days, the air tends to sit still rather than move, so the smog builds up and lingers. That means people end up breathing in more of it for longer stretches of time.

Breathing ground-level ozone irritates the lungs and airways. It can cause shortness of breath, trigger asthma attacks, and make the lungs more vulnerable to infections from things like pollen and mold. Spikes in ground-level ozone have been linked to increased hospital visits for asthma, pneumonia, COPD, and allergies — and even to premature death.

The toll is significant. Globally, ozone exposure contributed to an estimated 470,000 deaths in 2023, according to the Health Effects Institute's *State of Global Air 2025* report. In the United States alone, ozone was linked to roughly 14,000 COPD deaths in a single year — one of the highest rates among wealthy nations.

**Important note:** This ground-level ozone is different from the ozone layer high up in the atmosphere, which protects us from the sun's ultraviolet rays. Same molecule, very different effects depending on where it is.

---

## Wildfires and Dust Storms Pollute the Air

As the climate warms, droughts and wildfires are becoming more frequent in many places. Both create serious air quality problems.

**Wildfires** release a dangerous mix of tiny particles, toxic gases, and chemicals into the air. Breathing in wildfire smoke has been linked to shortness of breath, asthma attacks, and COPD flare-ups. It has also been associated with premature death and low birth weight in infants. And smoke doesn't stay near the fire — it can travel hundreds of miles, affecting people who live far from the flames. For example, the 2018 Camp Fire in Northern California created hazardous air conditions for roughly 7 million people in the San Francisco Bay Area, more than 150 miles from the fire.

**Droughts** create conditions ripe for dust storms, which fill the air with tiny particles that can irritate the lungs. During dry spells, these particles also hang around longer because there's less rain to wash them out of the air.

These tiny airborne particles — scientists call them "particulate matter" — are small enough to travel deep into your lungs and even enter your bloodstream. They are especially dangerous for people with existing respiratory conditions.

---

## Allergy Season Is Getting Longer and Worse

Warmer temperatures and higher levels of carbon dioxide in the air help plants grow more vigorously and produce more pollen. They can also extend the growing season, meaning plants release pollen over a longer period of time.

For example, the pollen season for ragweed — the most common allergy-triggering plant in the United States — grew significantly longer in parts of the central U.S. and Canada between 1995 and 2015.

More pollen in the air for more of the year means more chances of developing hay fever or triggering an asthma attack. Research also suggests that pollen interacts with air pollutants like smog and fine particles in ways that can make allergy symptoms and asthma attacks more severe, more frequent, or both.

---

## Some People Face Greater Risks

Poor air quality doesn't affect everyone equally. People in less developed countries and in cities around the world tend to breathe the most polluted air. But in any country, the people most at risk include:

- **Young children**, whose lungs are still developing
- **Older adults**, who are more likely to have existing health conditions
- **People with asthma, COPD, or other respiratory illnesses**

Wealth, access to health care, and public health resources also play a major role in how severely communities are affected.

---

## What You Can Do

**Check air quality before you go outside.** Federal, state, tribal, and local governments in the United States monitor air quality and publish it daily through the [Air Quality Index (AQI)](https://www.airnow.gov/). The AQI uses six levels to indicate when outdoor air may be harmful — especially for sensitive groups. On days when air quality is poor, staying indoors and keeping windows closed can reduce your exposure.

**Track pollen levels.** The [National Allergy Bureau](https://pollen.aaaai.org/#/) provides certified pollen and mold counts across the United States, so you can plan ahead during allergy season.

**Think long-term.** Reducing breathing problems from poor air quality ultimately means addressing their root cause: global warming. That starts with [cutting back on greenhouse gas emissions](https://www.nationalacademies.org/based-on-science/is-it-possible-to-achieve-net-zero-emissions).
