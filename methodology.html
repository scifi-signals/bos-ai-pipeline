How This Works

An AI pipeline that discovers trending science misinformation, matches it to authoritative research, and generates accessible, fact-checked articles. Every claim traces back to its source.

Question Discovery

Before any article is written, the pipeline identifies which questions need answering. This runs daily and works in four stages:

  • Signal detection — The Science Trend Monitor tracks 33+ RSS feeds, Bluesky science discussions, and science podcast transcripts. When a health or science topic starts trending, it becomes a candidate.
  • Misinformation filter — Claude evaluates each trending topic: Is there an active misinformation narrative? Are people making specific false claims? Topics without a clear misinfo angle are filtered out — the goal is to counter misinformation, not just explain science.
  • NASEM source matching — For each question that passes the filter, the pipeline searches a catalog of 1,300+ National Academies publications using keyword scoring and LLM reranking. Questions are only queued if relevant NASEM research exists to answer them.
  • Priority ranking — Questions are ranked by public health stakes, strength of the misinformation narrative, and quality of available NASEM sources. High-priority questions have active misinfo, clear public harm, and strong institutional evidence.

Each question in the discovery queue shows its misinformation narrative, why it matters, and every NASEM source that will be used — so you can evaluate the question before any article is generated.

Article Pipeline

1

Source Discovery

The pipeline identifies authoritative sources for each science question, prioritizing NASEM reports, IPCC assessments, and peer-reviewed research. Sources are ranked by a 4-tier system based on institutional authority and evidence quality.

TierSource Type
Tier 1NAS/NASEM reports, IPCC reports, peer-reviewed meta-analyses
Tier 2Government agencies (EPA, CDC, WHO), major health organizations
Tier 3Peer-reviewed individual studies, university research
Tier 4Science journalism, educational resources, advocacy organizations
2

Evidence Extraction

Claude reads each source document and extracts structured evidence: specific claims, supporting quotes, data points, strength assessments, and limitations. Long documents are chunked (~8,000 tokens with overlap) to ensure thorough coverage.

Each finding includes: claim, verbatim evidence quote, data points, strength rating (strong/moderate/limited), limitations, and source section reference.
3

Cross-Source Consensus

Claude analyzes all extracted evidence together to identify where sources agree, disagree, or provide complementary information. Claims are assigned consensus levels (strong, moderate, limited, conflicting) based on the weight and agreement of underlying evidence.

4

Article Generation

Claude generates an article following NASEM's "Based on Science" style guide. Articles target an 8th-grade reading level, open with a short answer, then walk through the evidence section by section. Every claim in the article must trace back to extracted evidence.

5

Independent Fact-Checking

GPT-4o independently verifies every factual claim in the article against the evidence package. Using a different model prevents self-confirmation bias. Each claim receives a verdict: Confirmed, Plausible, Unsupported, or Contradicted.

Reading level is also measured with Flesch-Kincaid, Flesch Reading Ease, and Gunning Fog metrics to verify the 8th-grade target is met.
6

Publication with Evidence Transparency

Every published article includes a companion evidence page showing the full evidence package: consensus analysis, fact-check results with individual verdicts, and collapsible source cards with per-source findings and original quotes. Readers can verify any claim back to its source.

Design Principles

  • Dual-model verification — Claude for analysis and generation, GPT-4o for independent fact-checking
  • Evidence traceability — every claim links to specific sources with exact quotes
  • Source hierarchy — institutional reports and meta-analyses weighted above individual studies
  • Accessible language — 8th-grade reading level, plain language, no jargon
  • Full transparency — evidence packages published alongside every article
Evidence sourced from the National Academies, IPCC, and peer-reviewed research.
