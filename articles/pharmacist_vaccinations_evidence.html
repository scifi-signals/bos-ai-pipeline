Evidence Package

How effective are pharmacists at providing vaccinations compared to doctors?

13 findings from 6 sources

Consensus Analysis

Pharmacies have become highly effective at delivering vaccinations and now serve as the dominant vaccination setting for adults, administering nearly half of flu vaccines and over two-thirds of COVID-19 vaccines compared to much lower rates at doctors' offices. However, pharmacies are used less frequently for pediatric vaccinations, and certain demographic groups continue to prefer medical settings.

  • 67.7% of all bivalent COVID-19 doses administered by retail pharmacies — CDC MMWR Federal Retail Pharmacy Program

CDC RespVaxView — Vaccination Settings Report 2023-24

— 6 findings
▶
Pharmacies are the most common vaccination setting, with higher usage rates than doctors' offices for all three vaccine types
Pharmacies or drug stores were the most common settings for flu (48.0%), updated COVID-19 (71.5%), and RSV vaccinations (81.7%); this was followed by a doctor's office (23.4%) for flu, clinics or health centers (9.2%) for updated COVID-19, and a doctor's office (7.9%) for RSV vaccinations.
strong Limitations: Data based on self-reported vaccination settings, not verified by medical records, subject to recall or social-desirability bias
The majority of adults receive vaccinations in nonmedical settings rather than medical settings
Overall, among vaccinees, 42.7% and 23.8% of adults aged ≥18 years received flu and updated COVID-19 vaccinations, and 16.9% of adults aged ≥60 years received RSV vaccinations in medical settings versus 57.3%, 76.2%, and 83.1% in nonmedical settings, respectively.
strong Limitations: Healthcare personnel vaccinated in medical settings might have reported workplace vaccination as nonmedical, potentially overestimating nonmedical percentages
Pharmacy vaccination usage has increased significantly over time, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic
Previous reports showed that during the 2014–15 and the 2018–19 flu seasons, 24.3% and 32.3% of vaccinated adults received their flu vaccination at a pharmacy, respectively, and this percentage increased to 38.5% in the 2020–21 season during the COVID-19 pandemic... Current data in this report from the 2023–24 flu season showed that 48.0% and 42.7% of all vaccinated adults received their flu vaccine in a pharmacy, and medical setting, respectively.
strong Limitations: Comparison across different survey methodologies and time periods
Medical settings remain more commonly used by certain demographic subgroups despite overall pharmacy dominance
However, medical settings remained the most common vaccination setting among some demographic subgroups, including Black, Hispanic, and American Indian or Alaskan Native adults, adults with a disability, uninsured adults, and those living in poverty.
strong Limitations: Some demographic subgroup sample sizes may be small, affecting reliability of estimates
Adults prefer doctor's offices over pharmacies when asked about vaccination preferences
Moreover, in a separate survey, adults reported that a doctor's office was their most preferred place to receive a vaccination, followed by a pharmacy or store.
limited Limitations: References separate survey with no details provided about methodology or sample size
The COVID-19 pandemic expanded pharmacist authority and demonstrated their vital role in vaccine administration
The COVID-19 pandemic provided evidence of the vital role pharmacists can play in administering vaccinations. During the COVID-19 pandemic, amendments to the Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness (PREP) Act expanded the authority of pharmacists, pharmacy technicians, and interns to administer vaccines, and those efforts made great contributions to COVID-19 vaccination uptake.
moderate Limitations: No specific quantitative data provided on the contribution to vaccination uptake
Tier 2  

CDC MMWR — Federal Retail Pharmacy Program COVID-19 Report

— 4 findings
▶
Retail pharmacies administered a higher proportion of COVID-19 bivalent vaccines compared to monovalent vaccines
FRPP partners administered 40.5 million bivalent COVID-19 doses, representing more than two thirds (67.7%) of all bivalent COVID-19 doses administered across the United States and its territories and freely associated states. In comparison, previous data show that FRPP partners administered 45% of monovalent doses during February 11, 2021–January 31, 2022
strong Limitations: COVID-19 vaccination coverage estimates were not possible using data from this analysis because unique persons vaccinated could not be identified
Parents report higher trust in regular clinics compared to pharmacies for pediatric vaccination
Surveys conducted during September 2021 found that parents reported a higher level of trust when vaccinating their child at their regular clinic (63%), compared with vaccination at 1) a local pharmacy (34%), 2) a clinic different from their regular one (30%), 3) school with the parent present (25%), 4) temporary mass vaccination clinic (25%), and 5) school without the parent present (15%)
moderate Limitations: Survey data from specific time period during COVID-19 pandemic
Pharmacies administered lower proportions of vaccines to children compared to adults
FRPP partners administered the highest proportion of bivalent vaccine doses to adults, with similar percentages of doses administered to adults in all age groups (range = 69.3%–70.6%). FRPP partners administered a lower proportion of bivalent doses to children aged 5–11 years (33.6%) and 6 months–4 years (5.9%)
strong Limitations: FRPP data only include vaccinated children aged ≥3 years, unlike the all-provider data, which included vaccinations administered to children and infants as young as age 6 months
Pharmacy vaccination accessibility factors contributed to higher vaccine administration rates
Factors that might have contributed to the higher proportion of bivalent compared with monovalent COVID-19 vaccines doses administered by FRPP partners include a higher level of awareness of COVID-19 vaccine availability at pharmacies, ease of accessibility (e.g., extended hours of operation, walk-in and scheduled appointments, and geographically convenient locations)
limited Limitations: Factors are speculative and not definitively established

NASPA — State Pharmacist Vaccination Authority (2025)

— 3 findings
▶
Children who received vaccines in pharmacies had similar odds of having timely well-child visits compared to those who received vaccines in primary care settings
A recent study published in Health Affairs Scholar examined whether pharmacy-based vaccinations for children aged 4–17 years led to delays in well-child visits. The findings indicated that the odds of having a timely well-child visit were similar between children who received vaccines in pharmacies and those who received them in primary care settings.
moderate Limitations: Study only examined well-child visit timing, not vaccination effectiveness itself
Parents who choose pharmacy-based pediatric vaccinations may actually be more likely to obtain timely well-child visits
This suggests that guardians or parents who choose pharmacy-based pediatric vaccinations do not forgo well-child visits and may actually be more likely to obtain a timely well-child visit.
limited Limitations: This is presented as a suggestion rather than a definitive finding
