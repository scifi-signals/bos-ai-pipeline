Does global warming cause breathing problems?

Global warming causes breathing problems through multiple pathways: it increases ground-level ozone that triggers respiratory disease, produces more wildfire smoke that damages lungs, and creates conditions that worsen asthma and allergies.

The Short Answer

Yes, global warming directly and indirectly causes breathing problems for millions of people worldwide. Higher temperatures increase the formation of ground-level ozone, a pollutant that damages the lungs and triggers asthma attacks. Warmer conditions also lead to more frequent and intense wildfires that produce harmful smoke, while creating conditions that favor airborne allergens like pollen and fungal spores. These impacts are already measurable and are projected to worsen as temperatures continue to rise.

Hot Weather Creates Dangerous Air Pollution

You've probably seen haze hanging over a city on a hot day. That haze often contains ground-level ozone, a pollutant that forms when sunlight and heat combine with emissions from cars, power plants, and industrial sources. As global warming increases temperatures, this process accelerates.

According to the IPCC's comprehensive assessment, even small increases in ground-level ozone concentrations can affect health. Research tracking 66 U.S. cities over 18 years (1982-2000) found that tropospheric ozone levels were significantly associated with cardiopulmonary mortality. The health risks persist even when ozone levels meet current EPA standards of 0.08 parts per million over 8 hours.

The global toll is substantial. Scientists estimate that ozone exposures caused 150,000 premature deaths worldwide in 2010. As temperatures rise, many air pollution models project increased ozone production, especially in and around urban areas where millions of people live and work.

Climate change modeling shows this problem will worsen. One study projected a 7.3% increase in summer ozone-related asthma emergency department visits for children in New York City by the 2020s compared to the 1990s, according to the IPCC assessment.

Wildfire Smoke Affects Millions for Months

The massive wildfire plumes you see on satellite images carry more than just dramatic visuals—they transport particulate matter and toxic substances that can affect large numbers of people for days to months. Climate change increases fire risk through higher temperatures combined with drought conditions, creating the perfect recipe for dangerous air quality.

During the 2010 Russian heat wave and wildfires, Moscow recorded air pollution levels between 430 and 900 micrograms per cubic meter of PM10 particles on most days, with occasional spikes reaching 1,500 micrograms per cubic meter. To put this in perspective, the World Health Organization's air quality guidelines recommend no more than 50 micrograms per cubic meter for PM10.

The health impact extends far beyond individual fire events. One global study estimated that air pollution from forest fires causes 339,000 deaths per year worldwide, with estimates ranging from 260,000 to 600,000 deaths annually. The regions most affected are sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia, according to IPCC findings.

Research has also revealed a concerning interaction effect: during the 2003 European heat wave, scientists found that possibly 50% of the deaths could have been associated with ozone exposure rather than heat itself, showing how multiple climate-related factors can compound breathing problems.

Warmer Weather Worsens Allergies and Asthma

If you've noticed longer or more intense allergy seasons, you're experiencing another way global warming affects breathing. Warmer conditions generally favor the production and release of airborne allergens such as fungal spores and plant pollen, consequently affecting asthma and other allergic respiratory diseases like allergic rhinitis.

Studies have documented clear connections between increasing grass pollen concentrations and more frequent ambulance calls for asthma symptoms, with a time lag of 3 to 5 days between pollen exposure and emergency responses. Children are particularly susceptible to most allergic diseases, making this a growing concern for families.

The mechanism is straightforward: higher temperatures extend growing seasons and can increase the amount of pollen that plants produce. Some plants also produce more potent allergens under elevated carbon dioxide levels, though this represents just one study's findings at very high CO2 concentrations.

Some People Face Greater Risks

Older adults face heightened vulnerability because they are more likely to suffer from health conditions that limit the body's ability to respond to stressors such as heat and air pollution. Children also face disproportionate risks, particularly from ozone and allergen exposures, with their developing respiratory systems making them more susceptible to long-term damage.

People who work outdoors—including agricultural and construction workers in tropical developing countries—are among the most exposed to climate-related breathing problems. More than half of all non-household labor hours occur outdoors, placing these workers at heightened risk of heat strain and exposure to poor air quality.

Geographic location matters significantly. Urban areas face higher ozone formation risks due to concentrated pollution sources and heat island effects. Regions prone to wildfires, including parts of the western United States, Australia, and fire-prone areas of sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia, experience the most severe impacts from smoke-related air pollution.

Economic factors also play a role. Households that burn solid fuels for cooking and heating face 3.5 million premature deaths annually from household air pollution, representing 4.4% of the global burden of disease. An additional 0.5 million deaths result from household cookfuel's contribution to outdoor air pollution.

What You Can Do

Monitor air quality daily through apps like AirNow or local weather services, especially during hot weather and wildfire season. Limit outdoor activities when air quality is poor.

Protect your indoor air by keeping windows closed during pollution episodes and considering air purifiers with HEPA filters. Change HVAC filters regularly and ensure proper ventilation.

Support clean energy transitions in your community. The IPCC found that climate mitigation measures could prevent 1.3 million premature deaths by 2050 through reduced air pollution exposure.

Manage allergies proactively by tracking pollen forecasts and taking medications before symptoms worsen. Keep windows closed during high pollen days and shower after spending time outdoors.

Advocate for policy changes that address both climate change and air quality. Reducing emissions of climate-altering pollutants like black carbon and methane could prevent 0.7 to 4.6 million premature deaths through PM2.5 reduction alone.
