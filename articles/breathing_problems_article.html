Does global warming cause breathing problems?

Global warming does cause breathing problems through several interconnected pathways, including increased ground-level ozone formation that triggers respiratory disease, more frequent wildfires producing harmful smoke, and rising allergen levels that worsen asthma.

The Short Answer

Yes, global warming directly contributes to breathing problems. Climate change worsens air quality by increasing ozone pollution, creates more wildfire smoke, and boosts allergen production — all of which harm respiratory health. The evidence shows these effects are already happening and will likely intensify as temperatures continue to rise.

Rising Temperatures Create Toxic Air

You've probably noticed that air quality alerts become more common on hot summer days. This happens because higher temperatures act like a chemical catalyst, speeding up the formation of ground-level ozone — a harmful air pollutant that forms when sunlight reacts with emissions from cars and power plants.

According to the IPCC AR5 WGII Chapter 11, many air pollution models project increased ozone production, especially within and surrounding urban areas, if temperatures rise. Even small increases in atmospheric concentrations of ground-level ozone may affect health, and research shows that levels meeting the U.S. EPA 8-hour regulation (0.08 ppm over 8 hours) were associated with increased risk of premature mortality.

The health impacts are substantial. In an analysis of 66 U.S. cities with 18 years of follow-up (1982–2000), tropospheric ozone levels were found to be significantly associated with cardiopulmonary mortality, according to the IPCC report. Globally, ozone exposure caused an estimated 150,000 premature deaths in 2010.

Climate projections suggest these problems will worsen. The IPCC assessment notes that in general, all-cause mortality related to ozone is expected to increase in the USA and Canada. One study projected a 7.3% increase in summer ozone-related asthma emergency department visits for children (0 to 17 years) across New York City by the 2020s compared to the 1990s.

Wildfires Fill the Air with Dangerous Particles

Climate change doesn't just affect invisible gases — it also creates visible threats. Heat waves and drought conditions make wildfires more likely and more intense, sending clouds of harmful particles into the air that can affect breathing for months.

According to the IPCC report, wildfires, which occur more commonly following heat waves and drought, release particulate matter and other toxic substances that may affect large numbers of people for days to months. The health toll is enormous: one study of worldwide premature mortality attributable to air pollution from forest fires estimated there were 339,000 deaths per year, with a range from 260,000 to 600,000.

During extreme events, air pollution from fires can reach dangerous levels. During Moscow's 2010 heat wave and wildfire crisis, the highest 24-hour pollution levels recorded were between 430 and 900 μg m⁻³ PM10 most days, but occasionally reached 1,500 μg m⁻³, according to the IPCC assessment. For comparison, WHO air quality guidelines recommend PM10 levels not exceed 50 μg m⁻³ — meaning Moscow's air was up to 30 times more polluted than safe levels.

The combination of heat and air pollution creates a particularly dangerous situation for breathing. Research on the European summer heat wave of 2003 found that possibly 50% of the deaths could have been associated with ozone exposure rather than the heat itself, as reported in the IPCC study.

More Pollen and Allergens Make Asthma Worse

If you have asthma or allergies, you may have noticed that pollen seasons seem to be getting longer and more intense. This isn't just your imagination — it's another way climate change affects breathing.

The IPCC report explains that warmer conditions generally favor the production and release of airborne allergens, such as fungal spores and plant pollen, and consequently, there may be an effect on asthma and other allergic respiratory diseases such as allergic rhinitis.

Studies have documented this connection directly. Research shows that increasing concentrations of grass pollen lead to more frequent ambulance calls due to asthma symptoms, with a time lag of 3 to 5 days, according to the IPCC assessment. Children are particularly susceptible to most allergic diseases, making them especially vulnerable to these climate-driven changes.

Some People Face Greater Risks

Climate change doesn't affect everyone's breathing equally. Children are particularly vulnerable to air pollution and allergen increases because their lungs are still developing and they breathe more air per pound of body weight than adults. The IPCC report notes that children are particularly susceptible to most allergic diseases.

Older adults also face heightened risks. According to the IPCC assessment, older people are more likely to suffer from health conditions that limit the body's ability to respond to stressors such as heat and air pollution.

Geographic location matters too. The regions most affected by forest fire air pollution are sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia, while urban areas face the greatest challenges from increased ozone formation. People who work outdoors — including agricultural and construction workers in tropical developing countries — face some of the highest exposures to both heat and air pollution.

What You Can Do

Check daily air quality before spending time outdoors. Use the AirNow website to check your local Air Quality Index and plan activities accordingly.

Monitor pollen levels if you have asthma or allergies. The National Allergy Bureau provides daily pollen counts to help you prepare for high-allergen days.

Stay indoors during poor air quality days with windows closed and air conditioning or air purifiers running. This is especially important for children, older adults, and people with existing respiratory conditions.

Support climate solutions that reduce air pollution. The IPCC report shows that climate mitigation measures could prevent 1.3 million premature deaths by 2050 through reduced air pollution exposure.

Talk to your doctor about how climate change might affect your respiratory health, especially if you have asthma, COPD, or other breathing conditions.
