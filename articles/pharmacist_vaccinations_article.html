How effective are pharmacists at providing vaccinations compared to doctors?

Pharmacies have become the dominant vaccination setting for adults in the United States, now administering nearly half of all flu shots and over two-thirds of COVID-19 vaccines — far outpacing doctors' offices.

The Short Answer

Pharmacists have proven highly effective at delivering vaccines and now serve as the primary vaccination providers for American adults. According to CDC data, pharmacies administered 48% of flu vaccines, 72% of COVID-19 vaccines, and 82% of RSV vaccines in 2023-24, compared to just 23%, 9%, and 8% respectively at doctors' offices. This shift represents a dramatic increase from 24% pharmacy usage in 2014-15, demonstrating pharmacists' growing role in public health.

Pharmacies Have Become Vaccination Leaders

Walk into any pharmacy today and you'll likely see signs advertising flu shots and COVID-19 boosters. This wasn't always the norm, but pharmacies have transformed into vaccination hubs over the past decade.

The numbers tell a striking story. According to the CDC's 2023-24 vaccination survey, pharmacies administered 48% of all flu vaccines to adults, while doctors' offices handled only 23.4%. For COVID-19 vaccines, the gap is even wider — pharmacies gave 71.5% compared to just 9.2% at doctors' offices. RSV vaccines show the most dramatic difference, with pharmacies providing 81.7% versus 7.9% at doctors' offices.

This represents a complete reversal from traditional vaccination patterns. During the 2014-15 flu season, only 24.3% of adults received their flu shot at a pharmacy. That percentage climbed to 32.3% by 2018-19, then jumped to 38.5% during the 2020-21 pandemic season before reaching today's 48%.

The COVID-19 pandemic played a crucial role in this transformation. Emergency expansions of pharmacist authority under the Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness Act allowed pharmacists and pharmacy technicians to administer vaccines more broadly. The Federal Retail Pharmacy Program administered 40.5 million bivalent COVID-19 doses, representing 67.7% of all bivalent doses nationwide — a significant increase from the 45% of monovalent doses pharmacies handled earlier in the pandemic.

Overall, most adults now receive their vaccines in nonmedical settings. The CDC found that 57.3% of flu vaccines, 76.2% of COVID-19 vaccines, and 83.1% of RSV vaccines were administered outside traditional medical offices.

Why Adults Choose Pharmacies

Several factors explain why adults increasingly turn to pharmacies for vaccinations rather than scheduling appointments with their doctors.

Convenience tops the list. Pharmacies typically offer extended hours, walk-in appointments, and geographically convenient locations. You can get vaccinated while picking up prescriptions or running errands, without needing to take time off work for a medical appointment.

Accessibility also matters. Many pharmacies accept walk-ins alongside scheduled appointments, reducing barriers that might prevent people from getting vaccinated. During the COVID-19 pandemic, this accessibility proved vital for maintaining vaccination coverage when medical offices faced capacity constraints.

However, preferences don't always match behavior. When surveyed about their preferred vaccination location, adults still report preferring doctors' offices over pharmacies. This suggests that while people value the convenience pharmacies offer, they maintain some preference for traditional medical settings when given the choice.

The pharmacy advantage becomes particularly apparent during public health emergencies. The established network of retail pharmacies provided crucial infrastructure for rapid vaccine distribution during COVID-19, demonstrating pharmacists' ability to scale up vaccination services quickly when needed.

Children Present a Different Picture

While pharmacies dominate adult vaccinations, the story changes dramatically for children. Parents show much stronger preferences for traditional medical settings when vaccinating their kids.

A September 2021 survey found that 63% of parents trusted their regular clinic for child vaccinations, compared to only 34% who trusted local pharmacies. This trust gap translates into usage patterns — pharmacies administered only 33.6% of bivalent COVID-19 vaccines to children aged 5-11 years and just 5.9% to children aged 6 months to 4 years, compared to 69.3-70.6% for adults.

Parents' preference for medical settings likely reflects several factors. Pediatric vaccination schedules are more complex, often requiring tracking of multiple vaccines over time. Parents may value the comprehensive care approach of pediatricians who can address questions about development, side effects, and vaccine scheduling during the same visit.

Important note: Research suggests that children who receive vaccines at pharmacies don't miss out on important medical care. A study published in Health Affairs Scholar found that children aged 4-17 who got vaccinated at pharmacies had similar odds of timely well-child visits compared to those vaccinated in primary care settings. This indicates that pharmacy-based vaccination doesn't replace necessary pediatric care.

The data limitations for very young children also reflect practical constraints — many pharmacies have age restrictions for vaccination services, and parents of infants and toddlers may prefer the specialized pediatric expertise available in medical offices.

Some People Face Greater Risks

Despite the overall shift toward pharmacy vaccination, certain groups continue to rely more heavily on medical settings, potentially due to access barriers or trust preferences.

Racial and ethnic minorities show higher rates of medical setting usage. According to CDC data, 52.4% of Black adults, 55.5% of Hispanic adults, and 59.3% of American Indian/Alaska Native adults received flu vaccines at medical settings rather than pharmacies. This pattern suggests that community trust, cultural factors, or access issues may influence vaccination location choices.

Uninsured adults also use medical settings more frequently, with 58.5% getting flu vaccines at doctors' offices or clinics. This could reflect different insurance coverage patterns for pharmacy services or the availability of sliding-scale fee programs at community health centers.

Adults with disabilities similarly rely more on medical settings, possibly due to accessibility needs or the preference to receive vaccinations alongside other medical care. These patterns highlight the continued importance of ensuring vaccine access through multiple channels.

Geographic factors also matter. Rural areas may have fewer pharmacy options, while urban areas with robust pharmacy networks show higher rates of pharmacy-based vaccination. The CDC noted that some demographic subgroups had small sample sizes in their surveys, which could affect the reliability of these estimates.

What You Can Do

Check your insurance coverage for vaccination at different locations. Most insurance plans cover vaccines at both pharmacies and medical offices, but copayments or coverage details may vary.

Consider your scheduling needs when choosing where to get vaccinated. If you prefer walk-in service or need evening or weekend hours, pharmacies often provide more flexible options than medical offices.

Ask about combination visits if you're due for multiple vaccines. Some pharmacies can administer several vaccines during one visit, while medical offices might better coordinate with your overall health record.

Discuss with your doctor if you have complex medical conditions or take multiple medications. Your physician may want to coordinate vaccination timing with other treatments or monitor for specific interactions.

For children, consider your comfort level and your child's needs. While both settings provide safe, effective vaccination, pediatric medical offices offer specialized expertise in child development and comprehensive care coordination.

Keep your vaccination records updated regardless of where you receive vaccines. Both pharmacies and medical offices should provide documentation, but maintaining your own records ensures continuity of care.
