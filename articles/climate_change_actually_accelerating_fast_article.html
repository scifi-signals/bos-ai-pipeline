Is climate change actually accelerating as fast as scientists claim?

Climate change is happening more than 10 times faster than the end of the last ice age, with most warming occurring in just the past 50 years.

The Short Answer

Yes, climate change is accelerating at rates that match or exceed what scientists have claimed. The current warming is happening more than 10 times faster than natural climate changes in Earth's past. Most of the 1°C (1.8°F) of global warming since 1900 has occurred in the last five decades, with over half of all human-caused carbon dioxide increases happening since 1970.

The Speed of Change Is Unprecedented

Think about how long it took Earth to warm up after the last ice age ended. That process took about 7,000 years. Now compare that to today's warming, which is happening in just 200 years.

According to the Climate Change: Evidence and Causes: Update 2020 report, current warming is more than 10 times faster than the ice age warming. This makes it much harder for people and nature to adapt to the changes.

The numbers tell a clear story. Carbon dioxide in our air has jumped by more than 40% since the Industrial Revolution started. More than half of that increase happened since 1970, according to the Climate Change: Evidence and Causes: Update 2020 report.

Today's carbon dioxide levels exceed 400 parts per million. This is higher than any time in the past million years. For 800,000 years before the 1900s, carbon dioxide stayed between 170 and 300 parts per million. The jump to over 400 parts per million in just 200 years is remarkable.

Temperature Records Show Rapid Recent Warming

Global temperatures have risen by about 1°C (1.8°F) since 1900, reports the Climate Change: Evidence and Causes: Update 2020. But most of this warming didn't happen gradually over the whole century.

Much of this temperature rise occurred in just the last five decades. This timing matches perfectly with when carbon dioxide levels started climbing most quickly. The 1970s marked a turning point when both greenhouse gas emissions and warming began to speed up.

This warming has brought many other changes with it. Ocean temperatures have risen. Sea levels have climbed higher. Arctic sea ice has shrunk dramatically. Heat waves have become more frequent and intense. All of these changes point to a climate system that is shifting rapidly.

Real-World Impacts Are Already Accelerating

Climate change isn't just about future threats. The impacts are happening right now and speeding up in many places around the world.

Thousands of species have already changed where they live, when they reproduce, or other key traits in response to climate shifts, according to the Abrupt Impacts of Climate Change report. This shows that even the natural world is struggling to keep up with the pace of change.

In the Arctic, permafrost thaw is causing serious problems for buildings and roads. Over the past 40 years, the ground's ability to support weight has dropped by more than 20% across large Arctic areas, reports the Abrupt Impacts of Climate Change. In some Russian cities, 10% to 80% of buildings have become unsafe due to ground instability.

On the Tibetan Plateau, ground temperatures have risen by 0.5°C in just 30 years. This has damaged roads, buildings, and other structures as the frozen ground underneath them thaws and shifts.

Some Places Face Greater Risks

Arctic regions are warming faster than anywhere else on Earth. This puts Arctic communities at the highest risk from rapid climate change. Indigenous peoples who depend on sea ice for hunting and travel face major disruptions to their way of life.

Small island nations and coastal communities worldwide are seeing faster sea level rise and more intense storms. These places often lack the resources to adapt quickly to changing conditions.

Farmers in dry regions and at low latitudes face declining crop yields as temperatures rise and rainfall patterns shift. Meanwhile, some northern areas may see farming benefits from longer growing seasons and higher carbon dioxide levels.

What You Can Do

Stay informed about climate science from trusted sources like the National Academies of Sciences. The evidence continues to evolve as scientists gather more data.

Support policies that reduce greenhouse gas emissions quickly. The faster we cut emissions, the less severe future warming will be.

Prepare for changes in your region. Even with emissions cuts, some additional warming is already locked in due to past emissions.

Share accurate information with others. Many people still don't understand how fast climate change is happening compared to natural changes in Earth's past.
